That was the acquisition of Slack Technologies —a messaging and collaboration platform for businesses that is popular but has yet to turn a profit. At $27.7 billion, Slack was nearly twice the size of Salesforce’s second-largest deal, which was the acquisition of Tableau Software in mid-2019. The deal also capped a run in which Salesforce acquired 28 companies in a five-year span, with reported values totaling $54.3 billion, according to FactSet.
Slack, in other words, was just the latest in a long string of deals that have grown more expensive over the years. But investors have taken a particularly dim view of the transaction. Salesforce shares have shed nearly 10% of their value since The Wall Street Journal first reported the talks between the two companies on Nov. 25. That contrasts with a 6% gain for the S&P 500 Software & Services Group and a 17% rise by the BVP Nasdaq Emerging Cloud Index over the same period. The price tag represented about 26 times Slack’s projected revenue for the next 12 months—more than twice the multiple paid for Tableau—and Salesforce didn’t even face competition on the deal, according to regulatory filings detailing the background of the merger.
That provides a challenging setup coming into Salesforce’s fiscal fourth-quarter results, scheduled for Thursday afternoon. The quarter is typically a strong period for the business, historically representing at least 45% of the company’s billings for the full fiscal year. But investors have been concerned about the rationale for the Slack deal, and the impact it will have on Salesforce’s margins. Slack is projected to show about $47 million in operating losses for the fiscal year ended January. An investor survey by Bernstein Research in December showed 84% of respondents believed the deal to be “value destructive” for Salesforce investors.
Salesforce already included Slack in its initial projection of 21% revenue growth for the fiscal year ending next January, so the potential for further upside from top-line growth guidance may be limited.

2021-02-24 06:03:00 Markets Why Higher Rates Are Hammering Big Tech Technology companies that prospered over the course of the pandemic have less appeal as the economy recovers Rising long-term Treasury yields ultimately might not pose much of a problem for most stocks. But the bout of indigestion that higher yields are already giving to some of the past year’s biggest winners—shares of companies including Amazon.com , Apple , Tesla and Microsoft —could prove more lasting.
Some of that is because these companies’ steep valuations make them vulnerable to rising yields. But it also stems from how, even as the Covid-19 pandemic wreaked havoc, those stocks did so well.
With the daily tally of new Covid-19 cases falling rapidly, millions of vaccine doses going into arms each week and another round of government support looking as if it is on its way, a return to something like normal in the months ahead is looking more and more likely. And with that prospect of recovery, bond investors have become less enamored with the safety of long-term Treasurys, pushing rates higher. The 10-year note’s yield is now 1.36%, compared with 0.88% just three months ago, marking its highest level in a year.
For most U.S. companies, the all-clear signal that the Treasury market has started sounding is a good thing. As Americans get back to doing things like eating out at restaurants, heading out on vacations and going to the mall, sales could flourish. Considering how much money has built up in a lot of people’s bank accounts during the pandemic and how much pent-up demand there is for all the things they did before the pandemic, there could be a bit of a boom.
For the shares of those companies, on the other hand, the rise in long-term rates provides a bit of a challenge: The higher that yields go, the more competitive bonds are with stocks. The challenge is hardly insurmountable, however, considering that the better economy that yields are heralding makes for better profits. That is why as often as not in recent years, periods of rising Treasury yields coincide with stocks rising, too.
